# Lesson 2.2: Reasoned Prompting (Chain of Thought)

Large Language Models do not calculate answers before outputting text. Because they predict the next token on the fly, if you ask a model a complex logic, math, or translation problem and demand a direct answer, it will often output the wrong answer instantly.

To fix this, we use **Reasoned Prompting (Chain-of-Thought)** to force the model to calculate its reasoning step-by-step before arriving at the final answer.

---

## 1. What is Chain-of-Thought (CoT)?

**Chain-of-Thought (CoT)** prompts include explicit steps showing *how* to solve a problem. By writing out the reasoning path in the prompt's examples, the model learns to generate a similar reasoning path when tackling new questions.

### Simple Zero-Shot CoT: "Think Step by Step"
If you don't have examples, you can trigger basic reasoning by adding a magic suffix to your prompt:
> **"Let's think step by step."**

This simple phrase forces the model to allocate output tokens for reasoning, which dramatically improves performance on math word problems, logical deductions, and data parsing.

---

## 2. CoT Prompt Construction

Let's test this in the sandbox below. Notice how instructing the model to show its calculations reduces errors:

import PromptPlayground from './components/PromptPlayground.jsx';

<PromptPlayground
  systemInstruction="You are a helpful logic tutor."
  variables={["puzzle_input", "reasoning_instruction"]}
  template="PUZZLE:
{puzzle_input}

INSTRUCTION:
{reasoning_instruction}
"
/>

Try setting `puzzle_input` to:
> *"A farmer has 15 sheep. All but 8 die. How many are left?"*

And set `reasoning_instruction` to:
> Option A: *"Answer with a single number."* (High chance of error)
>
> Option B: *"Let's think step by step. Explain your reasoning first, then conclude with the final number."* (Correct logic)

## 3. Tree of Thoughts (ToT)

For extremely complex planning or strategic tasks (like optimizing a project timeline or outlining a research paper structure), you can ask the model to evaluate multiple branches of thoughts:

1. **Generate hypotheses:** "Create 3 distinct approaches to solve this problem."
2. **Evaluate viability:** "For each approach, list its pros, cons, and probability of success."
3. **Select the optimal path:** "Choose the best path and write the final solution."

This mimics human brain storming and prevents the model from locking into a bad initial assumption.
